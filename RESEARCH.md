## Primary reference for settled science

Unless a specific citation is given, every claim in Part A traces to:

Alberts B, Johnson A, Lewis J, Raff M, Roberts K, Walter P. *Molecular Biology of the Cell*, 4th edition. New York: Garland Science; 2002. Hosted free by the U.S. National Library of Medicine (NIH) on the NCBI Bookshelf: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/

Why this source: it is authoritative, free to the public, and legally clean (posted by agreement with the publisher). The 2002 edition is old, but the structure and mechanism of a mitochondrion are settled science that has not changed since then, so an older edition is appropriate here rather than a compromise. One quirk: the Bookshelf copy is searchable but not freely page-browsable, which is fine for citing.

## Part A — Settled science (core structure & function)

- ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is the cell's main energy currency. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- ATP's structure is a core called adenosine (the base adenine joined to a ribose sugar) plus a tail of three phosphate groups bonded in a row. The "tri" in triphosphate refers to those three phosphates. [Source: Physiology, Adenosine Triphosphate, StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK553175/ ; Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- ATP releases usable energy when its outermost phosphate is split off, a reaction called hydrolysis (splitting by adding water) that turns ATP into ADP (adenosine diphosphate, two phosphates) plus one free inorganic phosphate. [Source: Physiology, Adenosine Triphosphate, StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK553175/ ; Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]
  - Honesty note for copy: do not say "energy is stored in the high-energy bond and released when the bond breaks," which is standard shorthand but imprecise (breaking a bond on its own costs energy; the net energy comes from the reaction settling into more stable products). Say "energy is released when ATP is split into ADP plus phosphate."

- The ATP/ADP cycle is reversible: adding a phosphate back onto ADP, using energy from food, regenerates ATP. In the mitochondrion this recharge step is run by ATP synthase, which is exactly the reaction Scene 6 depicts. [Source: Physiology, Adenosine Triphosphate, StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK553175/ ; Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Mitochondria do not create energy. They release the chemical energy already stored in the bonds of food molecules and repackage it into ATP, a form the cell can spend. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- The first stage of extracting energy from glucose is glycolysis, and it happens in the cytoplasm, outside the mitochondrion, before anything reaches the organelle. Glycolysis yields only about 2 ATP per glucose. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Full aerobic respiration, involving the mitochondrion, yields roughly 30 or more ATP per glucose (about 15x more than glycolysis alone). [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- The outer membrane is permeable to small molecules, which pass through channel proteins called porins. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- The inner membrane is highly selective and effectively sealed, which is what lets a proton gradient build up across it. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- The inner membrane is folded into structures called cristae, which increase surface area for the energy-producing machinery. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- The matrix (innermost space) contains mitochondrial DNA, ribosomes, and the enzymes of the citric acid (Krebs) cycle. The citric acid cycle is the stage that runs inside the mitochondrion; it breaks fuel down further and loads high-energy electrons onto carrier molecules for the electron transport chain. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- The electron transport chain moves electrons through a series of protein complexes embedded in the inner membrane and uses the energy released to pump protons from the matrix into the intermembrane space, creating a gradient. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Not every complex in the chain pumps protons. Complexes I, III, and IV pump; Complex II (also called succinate dehydrogenase) takes part in the chain but does not pump any protons across the membrane. [Source: Biochemistry, Electron Transport Chain — StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK526105/]

- Complex II is a second entry point into the chain: electrons from the citric acid cycle step in which succinate becomes fumarate enter here (carried by FADH2), bypassing Complex I. Because this route skips a proton-pumping step, it ultimately drives less ATP than electrons that enter at Complex I (carried by NADH). [Source: Biochemistry, Electron Transport Chain — StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK526105/]

- The gradient across the inner membrane is electrochemical: it has a concentration part (more protons on one side) and an electrical part (a genuine voltage, because a proton carries positive charge, so separating protons across the membrane separates positive from negative). The thin inner membrane holding separated charge on its two faces behaves like a charged capacitor, the core of a battery. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- ATP synthase is a rotary molecular motor. Protons flowing back across the inner membrane physically spin it, and that rotation drives the assembly of ATP (a process called chemiosmosis). It builds each ATP by joining two parts already present in the matrix, ADP and an inorganic phosphate; it does not create ATP from nothing. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Mitochondria contain their own small, circular DNA, separate from the cell's nuclear DNA. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Endosymbiotic theory: mitochondria descend from free-living bacteria that were engulfed by an ancestral cell over a billion years ago. This is why they still carry their own DNA. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Mitochondrial DNA is inherited maternally: you inherit essentially all of your mitochondria from your mother. (Use "essentially all," not "every single one," because rare paternal transmission has been reported and is debated. The maternal-inheritance story is the correct consensus to teach; the absolute is what to avoid.) [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Mitochondria help trigger apoptosis (programmed cell death), in part by releasing cytochrome c. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

- Beyond ATP, mitochondria contribute to heat generation, calcium storage, and steroid hormone synthesis. [Source: Molecular Biology of the Cell, 4th ed. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK21054/]

### Specific numbers (each independently sourced)

- Human heart muscle is roughly a quarter mitochondria by volume. Direct morphometry (physical measurement under an electron microscope) of biopsied human hearts found myocardium to be about 23% mitochondria, 59% myofibrils, and 18% cytoplasm. [Source: Schaper J et al., "Ultrastructural morphometric analysis of myocardium from dogs, rats, hamsters, mice, and from human hearts," Circulation Research, 1985. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3882260/]
  - **Correction note:** the earlier draft said "about a third." That figure (and the "30 to 40%" seen on wellness sites) is real for small, fast-hearted mammals like mice and shrews, not for humans. Across mammals the range is roughly 22 to 37%, with humans near the bottom. Use "about a quarter" for the human heart. [Cross-species range source: "Scaling of quantitative cardiomyocyte properties in the left ventricle of different mammalian species." https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11744323/]

- The human body turns over roughly its own weight in ATP per day. The standing amount of ATP in the body at any instant is small (sources put it in the tens to low hundreds of grams), so the daily body-weight figure comes from recycling: each ATP molecule is rebuilt and reused many hundreds of times a day. A classic estimate (Kornberg) puts daily turnover near 180 kg. [Source: Physiopedia summary citing Voet & Voet, Fundamentals of Biochemistry. https://www.physio-pedia.com/Adenosine_triphosphate_(ATP) ; Kornberg figure via https://arxiv.org/pdf/1204.6670]

- The human body contains on the order of 37 trillion cells (about 36 to 37 trillion in males, about 27 trillion in females). [Source: Human Reference Atlas cell-population paper. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12393406/]

- The number of mitochondria per cell varies enormously by tissue, from a few hundred in a quiet cell up to roughly 100,000 to 600,000 in a human egg cell. There is no rigorous count of total mitochondria in a human body; popular totals (such as "10 quadrillion") are rough estimates, not measurements. [Source, per-cell and oocyte range: Embryo Project Encyclopedia, "Mitochondrial DNA." https://embryo.asu.edu/pages/mitochondrial-dna-mtdna]

- Caution on counting: even "number of mitochondria per cell" is an oversimplification, because mitochondria fuse into dynamic networks rather than sitting as separate countable beans. State floors ("at least this many"), not a single hard total. [Source: "Modeling mitochondria, where are the numbers?" https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10883508/]

## Part B — Frontier science (for the "bigger story" beats)

*These are areas of active research. On the site they must sit AFTER a clearly marked threshold telling the visitor we are leaving settled textbook material and entering where science is still working things out.*

- Mitochondrial dysfunction is recognized as one of the hallmarks of aging, one of twelve interacting drivers in the current framework (not "the cause" of aging). [Source: López-Otín C, Blasco MA, Partridge L, Serrano M, Kroemer G, "Hallmarks of aging: An expanding universe," Cell, 2023;186(2):243-278. Free article: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36599349/]

- Endurance exercise stimulates the body to build more mitochondria in skeletal muscle (mitochondrial biogenesis). A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found a significant increase in PGC-1-alpha, the most consistently reported marker of this process, following endurance exercise. [Source: "The impact of exercise on mitochondrial biogenesis in skeletal muscle: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized trials," 2025. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40459444/]
  - Honesty note for copy: "train endurance and your body responds by building more mitochondria" is safe. Do not claim a single molecular "master switch" is required; that story is cleaner in popular accounts than in the lab.

- Faults in mitochondria cause real inherited disease. MELAS (mitochondrial encephalomyopathy, lactic acidosis, and stroke-like episodes) is one of the most common maternally inherited mitochondrial disorders, most often caused by the m.3243A>G mutation. It hits the most energy-hungry organs first (brain, heart, muscle). [Source: MELAS syndrome clinical review. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S109671921530024X ; StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK532959/]

- Primary mitochondrial disease collectively has a prevalence of about 1 in 5,000. [Source: "LNC-ing Genetics in Mitochondrial Disease," NCBI. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11587075/]
